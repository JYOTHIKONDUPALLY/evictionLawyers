What Is Eviction in England & Wales?

Eviction is the formal legal process for regaining possession of a property when a tenancy ends or obligations are not met. Landlords must comply with the Housing Act 1988, Housing Act 1996, and the Renters (Reform) Bill 2025.

Serving the correct notices and following proper procedures is essential to remain compliant.

Landlords should also maintain clear records of communication, payments, and any breaches of the tenancy agreement, as this evidence can be crucial if possession proceedings reach court. Seeking professional advice or assistance from a qualified letting agent or solicitor can help ensure the eviction process is handled lawfully, fairly, and efficiently, minimizing potential disputes or delays.

Step-by-Step Eviction Process

When a tenant fails to vacate the property after being served with a valid notice, the landlord must take the next step by applying to the court for possession. This formal process ensures you recover your property lawfully and in line with the Housing Act. The court will guide the matter through a series of stages, typically taking 6–12 weeks.

1
Serve Notice

Serve Section 21 or Section 8 notice to tenant with proper legal requirements met.

2
Wait for Expiry

2 weeks–2 months waiting period, depending on grounds and notice type served.

3
Issue Possession Claim

Accelerated or standard claim process filed with the appropriate court.

4
Court Review/Hearing

Paper review for accelerated claims or oral hearing for standard claims.

5
Possession Order

Court typically grants possession order within 14–28 days of hearing.

6
Bailiff Enforcement

If tenant does not vacate voluntarily, bailiff enforcement arranged.
